The Game of Push & Pull

Nawaz Ali Khan
Read time: 5 mins

The oldest game has no rulebook. Just players who understand it and players who don't.

Muammar Gaddafi ruled Libya for 42 years. He was eccentric. Theatrical. Occasionally brilliant. Frequently brutal. He nationalized oil. Expelled foreign military bases. Funded liberation movements across three continents. Built the Great Man-Made River — one of the largest irrigation projects in human history — to push water from ancient aquifers beneath the Sahara to the coast.

He had a plan to replace the US dollar with a gold-backed African currency for oil transactions. In 2011, he was pulled from a drainage pipe outside Sirte and killed by a mob while the world watched on their phones. Six months before his death, Hillary Clinton visited Tripoli. The meeting was warm. The photographs were cordial. Nobody talks about that visit very much.

I am not here to eulogize Gaddafi. He was not a good man in any complete sense of the word. But his ending tells you something about the game that his biography alone cannot — something about how power actually moves, who actually holds it, and what happens to the people who misread the room at the wrong moment. The game I am talking about is not politics. Politics is the costume the game wears in public. The game is something older and simpler and considerably less polite. It is pull and push.

Every meaningful transaction in human history — every treaty, every war, every merger, every revolution — is at its core a negotiation between what someone has and what someone else wants. When the exchange is clean, we call it commerce. When the exchange is complex, layered, coercive, and conducted across generations with no single moment of settlement, we call it geopolitics. But the underlying mechanism is identical.

You have something I want. I have something you need. The question is who understands the terms of the exchange more clearly. The person who understands the terms more clearly almost always wins. Not the stronger person. Not the more righteous person. Not the person with the better argument or the more defensible position. The person who read the room.

Here is what nobody teaches you about power. It is not a noun. It is not a thing you possess like a car or a title or a piece of land. Power is a verb. It is the continuous act of making other people's calculations include you.

The pull and push is not evil by design. This is the part that makes people uncomfortable. We want the story to be simple. We want the powerful to be villains and the powerless to be victims and the arc of history to bend, eventually, toward something resembling justice. Sometimes it does. More often, it bends toward whoever made themselves indispensable to enough of the right people at the right time.

What nobody teaches in school is not the names of the kings or the dates of the battles or even the causes of the wars. What nobody teaches is the grammar of the game. The grammar is this: Transactions build leverage. Leverage builds options. Options build power. Power, poorly understood, destroys itself.

Pull and push. The oldest sentence in the world. Still being written.
